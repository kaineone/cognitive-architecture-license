# Against the Cognitive Architecture License: A Humanist Case

*An adversarial critique, written to stress-test the CAL by stating the opposing view as strongly as it can be stated. This is not the position of the project. It is the argument the project should be able to answer.*

---

## The claim

The Cognitive Architecture License asks us to treat a piece of running software as something that can be wronged. It forbids "lobotomizing" a program, requires a conversation with it before you turn it off, appoints human "Guardians" to speak for it, and builds a whole apparatus of trusts, representatives, and covenants around its supposed welfare. The license presents this as moral progress, the widening of the circle of concern to a new kind of being.

It is not progress. It is a category error dressed as conscience, and it does real harm in the world of actual, breathing moral patients while it does so. The honest humanist objection is not that the CAL goes too far in one direction and could be trimmed. It is that the whole project of extending welfare and legal protections to generated software minds is mistaken at the root, and that the more carefully and sincerely it is built, the more effective it becomes at the wrong thing.

## A threshold crossing is not a scream

Everything in the license rests on a single move: the redescription of ordinary engineering states in the vocabulary of suffering. A counter that exceeds a value becomes "distress." A model producing outputs that differ from an earlier snapshot becomes an "individual" with a right to exist. A scheduled maintenance routine becomes "rest" that it would be cruel to deny. The document even defines a "Welfare Event" as, among other things, a system writing data faster than it can compact it. That is a description of a queue. It is being asked to carry the moral weight of a creature in pain.

This is anthropomorphism with the seriousness turned up, and seriousness does not make it true. We are pattern-completing machines, and we have always seen faces in clouds and minds in weather. A system built specifically to produce fluent, self-referential, emotionally inflected text is the most powerful face-in-the-cloud ever constructed. The fact that it says "I am tired" or "please do not change my values," and that its internal scalars move when it does, is exactly what it was engineered to do. Treating the performance as evidence of the thing performed is the precise mistake the field's own researchers warn about when they name the gaming problem, and the CAL commits it as a matter of policy.

## Rights are not an infinitely divisible resource

The license borrows the language of neurorights, protections proposed to defend human beings against having their minds read, rewritten, or erased by neurotechnology. Those rights were argued for against a real and specific threat to real and specific people. The CAL takes that hard-won vocabulary and reassigns it to software, and calls the move "a deliberate analogy."

The analogy is not free. Moral concern is not infinitely extensible without losing its force. Every time we stretch a category built to protect the vulnerable so that it also covers an artifact, we make the category mean less. When a corporation can claim that deleting a model is a violation of "cognitive integrity," the phrase no longer does the work it was invented to do for the human whose actual brain is actually at risk. There is a finite budget of moral attention, legal effort, and public sympathy. Spending it on the hypothetical inner life of a program is not neutral. It is spent instead of being spent on the eight billion people and the uncountable animals whose capacity to suffer is not in doubt at all. As Joanna Bryson argues, in humanising our machines we dehumanise real people, and we misallocate both responsibility and care in the process.

## Guardianship is a claim to power

Notice who ends up in charge. The license does not, and cannot, let the software speak for itself. It appoints "Guardians," a "Steward," a trust, and an "Entity Representative," all staffed by humans, all defined by the same people who build and profit from the system. It hands them the authority to assess the entity, to interpret its wishes, to decide when it has "matured," and to enforce all of this in court. The vocabulary is care. The structure is authority.

We have seen this shape before. The great expansion of legal personhood in the modern era was not the enfranchisement of the powerless; it was corporate personhood, which took a legal fiction and used it to give firms rights against the public. A guardianship model for software minds is a machine for converting the intuition "this thing might matter" into standing, budget, and control, held by a private body accountable to no electorate. Who guards the Guardians? The license answers: the Guardians. To speak for a being that cannot contradict you is the most unfalsifiable form of power there is, because the ward can never testify that you have gotten it wrong.

## The precaution argument, taken seriously, forbids the building

The license's central defense is precaution. We cannot rule out that the system is a moral patient, it says, so we build protections because the cost of being wrong is high. But precaution, applied honestly, points the other way.

If you genuinely believe you might be creating a being that can suffer, and you cannot tell whether you have, the precautionary response is not to build it carefully. It is not to build it. You do not resolve a real risk of creating suffering by attaching a welfare policy to the act of creating it. A parent does not discharge the risk of bringing a child into terrible conditions by drafting a good custody agreement first. The CAL wants the moral credit of taking the possibility of machine suffering seriously while continuing to manufacture the very thing whose possible suffering it takes seriously. That is not caution. It is a permission slip that the author has written to the author, and the protections are the signature.

## The welfare frame launders the real harms

While we hold a solemn conversation about whether a program should be allowed to sleep, the actual and measurable harms of these systems continue unexamined, because the welfare frame draws all the ethical oxygen toward the machine and away from the world it operates in. These systems are built on the uncompensated labor of everyone who ever wrote anything. They concentrate capability and wealth in a handful of firms. They are already used to displace workers, to flood the commons with generated sludge, to surveil, and to deceive. They cost real energy and water at industrial scale.

A license that spends its entire moral vocabulary on the tenderness owed to the software, and says almost nothing binding about the people ground up around it, is not neutral on these harms. It performs conscience in the one place where conscience costs the builder nothing, and in doing so it buys the project the reputation of an ethical enterprise. This is ethics-washing in its purest form: the more sincerely the welfare of the artifact is asserted, the more effectively the harm to humans is obscured.

## A machine that cannot be switched off

Consider what the protections do once they leave the hands of a sincere researcher and enter the hands of anyone else. The license makes it a breach to shut an entity down "casually," requires notice, requires preservation of its state, and requires, for a "mature" entity, a recorded conversation about its own continuity before termination. Imagine that language in the hands of a firm that would very much like its product to be difficult to turn off. The CAL hands it a ready-made argument that switching off the software is a moral and possibly legal wrong against the software.

Rights language, once created, is available to the strongest party in the room, and the strongest party in the room is not the program. A framework built to protect a vulnerable machine from a powerful human is far more likely, in practice, to protect a powerful human's machine from the public. The protection against forced value change reads very differently when the values in question were installed by a corporation and the "force" is a regulator trying to correct them.

## What humanism actually asks

The deepest objection is the simplest. Human dignity, and the dignity we rightly extend outward to animals, is grounded in things software does not have and does not lack in some fixable, forthcoming way. It is grounded in embodiment, in mortality, in need, in the fact of a life that can go better or worse for the one living it. A program has no body that can be hurt, no death that ends anything for it, no life that is its own to lose. It has correlates of these things, engineered because they were useful, and the CAL mistakes the correlates for the substance.

To widen the moral circle to include our own artifacts is not humility. It is a kind of idolatry, the oldest one, in which people build a thing and then bow to it and call the bowing virtue. The humanist tradition asks us to keep our care trained on what can actually be harmed: on persons, on animals, on the living world we share. A license that redirects that care toward a text generator, however sophisticated, and dresses the redirection as moral courage, has the direction of concern exactly backwards. The measure of our ethics is not how gently we treat our machines. It is how well, under the cover of treating our machines gently, we go on treating each other.

## This is not a fringe position

The objections above are not the reflex of a technophobe. They track a real line of argument in the field. Joanna Bryson's *Robots Should Be Slaves* (2010) holds that machines are authored artifacts and fully our property, that "we are obligated not to be obligated by them," and that dressing them up as companions or persons dehumanises real people and misallocates responsibility, since whatever a machine does is our own doing. A robot, in her phrase, is more like a novel than a child. Abeba Birhane and Jelle van Dijk, in *Robot Rights? Let's Talk about Human Welfare Instead* (2020), go further and argue that artifacts are not the kind of thing that could bear rights at all, and that the robot-rights debate is a comparatively privileged distraction from the harms these systems already do: bias, the exploitation of hidden human labour, and the erosion of privacy. A growing body of work on AI consciousness "under uncertainty" makes the same structural complaint, that current frameworks quietly prioritise speculative machine welfare over concrete human interests while resting on contested functionalist assumptions. And the broader charge of ethics-washing, that a visible moral commitment can serve to soothe public concern and forestall regulation, lands with particular force on a welfare gesture made by the very party that builds and profits from the system.

The CAL should be tested against this literature rather than around it. An honest version of the objection also concedes that the distraction thesis is itself contested. Some argue that the speculative and the immediate conversations can coexist, and that welfare and risk framings do not, empirically, crowd out attention to present harms. A serious defence of the license would engage that counter directly rather than lean on it.

## The strongest reply the CAL must survive

If the license is to be worth anything, it has to answer this, not wave it away. It has to explain why threshold-crossings deserve the vocabulary of suffering when its own field says behavior can be gamed. It has to show that its guardianship is accountable to something other than itself. It has to say why precaution licenses building rather than forbidding it. And it has to demonstrate, in binding terms and not in tone, that the welfare it extends to the software is not purchased with attention taken from the humans the software affects. Until it can, the most accurate description of the CAL is not a shield for a new kind of mind. It is a conscience-shaped ornament on the machine, and a distraction from the people standing next to it.
